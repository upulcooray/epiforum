Background

Background

An Overview of Existing Causal Inference Methodology

  • Most methods utilize conterfactual (potential outcomes) framework

I.e. manipulating the exposure for thesame observations and then compare outcomes under different exposures

  • Mostly, binary exposures are used [what if everyone exposed Vs what if everyone not exposed]

  • Known as static interventions

Some Limitations

  1. Positivity violations

  2. Unrealistic interventions

  3. Cannot depend on the observed exposure

  4. Policy implications of conterfactual extremes 🤔

  5. Forced to dichotomise the exposure

Other possibilities

🎯 Dynamic interventions

Hypothetical exposure level for participants is dependent on baseline covariates

Eg: setting a certain exposure level only for participants with low income level

🎯 Modified interventions

Setting participant to a new exposure level based on their observed (naturally occurring) value of the exposure

Eg: Change the level of exposure by 10%

Background

What does Modified Treatment Policy framework offer

  • A collection of nonparametric alternatives for defining and yielding effects from meaningful causal inference questions

  • Capable of estimating interventions with practical relevance such as reducing or increasing the exposure by a given amount

  • R package lmtp provides software implementation of these methods. (See the paper by Iván Díaz et al. titled “Nonparametric Causal Effects Based on Longitudinal Modified Treatment Policies” for further details)

  • lmtp package allows the user to handle:

    • Cross-sectional or longitudinal exposures

    • Binary, categorical, or continuous exposures

    • Exposures that are dependent on time

    • Time-dependent covariates

    • Multiple exposures

    • Binary, continuous, or time-to-event outcomes

    • Informative loss to follow-up/censoring

    • Competing risks

    • Survey weights

Methods

Methods

Data & the flow of participants

  • Data & Participants
  • Flow chart

  • Data: From the Japan Gerontological Evaluation Study (JAGES)

  • JAGES is an on-going nationwide cohort study for older adults in Japan

  • Data from the 2010 (baseline) and two subsequent follow-up surveys (2013 and 2016) were used

  • 52,053 included in the baseline survey

  • 24,872 individuals responded to all three waves

  • We included all (52,053) baseline participants with their follow-up status to obtain estimates accounted for censoring

  • Missing data (other than due to censoring) was imputed using multiple imputation (five datasets)

Methods

Study variables

  • Outcome
  • Exposure
  • Covariates
  • Hypothesized associations

  • Social participation in 2016

  • Participation in any of the activities;

    • hobby groups
    • sports clubs
    • senior citizens clubs
    • residence groups
    • or volunteer groups

  • Participation in any of these activities once a month or more frequently (vs. less frequently or never) was defined as indicative of social participation (1=participation, 0=non-participation)

  • The number of remaining natural teeth in 2010 and 2013 was used as a time-varying exposure

  • Self-reported number of teeth was recorded in four categories (i.e., >=20 teeth/ 10-19 teeth/ 1-9 teeth/ no teeth)

  • Time-varying nature of covariates were taken into account

  • Time-invariant covariates were;

    • age (range 65–99 years)
    • sex (male/female)
    • social participation in 2010 (outcome at the baseline)

  • Time-varying covariates were;

    • Annual household income
    • Self-rated health (very good/ good/ fair/ poor)
    • Denture use (yes/ no)
    • Marital status (married/ single, widowed or divorced)

Methods

Statistical analysis- main steps

Step 1: Defined the causal effect using MTP framework by shifting the observed exposure to different hypothetical scenarios

Step 2: Estimate the population level outcome (mean outcome) under different scenarios specified in the step

Targeted minimum loss-based estimation (TMLE) with weighted combination data adaptive regressions (machine learning algorithms) were used to estimate outcomes.

Step 3: Contrast the outcome estimates of hypothetical interventions against the outcome estimate under observed exposure level to calculate odds ratios.

Step 3- Contrast outcome estimates to obtain the causal estimand

Finally, TMLE estimates were contrasted against the outcome estimate under the actual observed exposure;

  • Observed vs scenario 1 (t4 → t3)

  • Observed vs scenario 2 (t4 & t3 → t2)

  • Observed vs scenario 3 (t4, t3, t2 → t1)

  • Observed vs scenario 4 (t1 → t2)

  • Observed vs scenario 5 (t1 & t2 → t3)

Results

Results

Contrasting outcome estimates

Contrast OR [95% CI] P value
Observed vs Scenario 1 0.94 [0.90-0.97] 0.001

Observed vs Scenario 4 1.04 [1.02-1.06] 0.000
Observed vs Scenario 5 1.11 [1.07-1.14] 0.000


  • A clear dose response effect of number of teeth on social participation was observed

  • In scenarios where tooth loss was mimicked, likelihood of social participation declined compared to factual(observed) level.

  • In scenarios where tooth gain* was mimicked, likelihood of social participation increased compared to factual(observed) level.

* practically, tooth gain can either be prosthetic treatments or tooth loss prevention
